I am a research scientist at General AI Lab, Horizon Robotics. I got my Bachelor of Computer Science from Peking University, and my Ph.D. of Electrical and Computer Engineering (ECE) from Purdue University. Previously, I worked at Baidu Research and Facebook AI Research.

My earlier research topics include object segmentation [ACMMM2010], language grounding from video [ACL2013,AAAI2015,JAIR2015,IJCV2017], video captioning [CVPR2016], embodied AI [ICLR2018,CoRL2018,arXiv2019], and the lottery ticket hypothesis [NeurIPS2019,ICLR2020].

Since joining Horizon Robotics in 2019, I've been working on reinforcement learning (RL) problems, including hierarchical RL [ICLR2021,NeurIPS2021], safe/constrained RL, and exploration. I am also an active maintainer of the RL framework ALF.

Research interests

After working on various topics of AI using deep learning, now as a researcher I'm no longer satisfied with applying deep learning, as a blackbox optimization tool, to a fixed dataset (no matter how large it is), for the sole purpose of learning statistical correlations \(f(x,y)\). For instance, \((x,y)\) could be \((image,label)\), \((video,sentence)\), \((sentence, sentence)\), or any other such combinations.

I personally don't believe that correlation will lead us to true AI, just as we "don't get to the moon by climbing a tree". Studying how to get to the tree top isn't that really exciting to me. Deep learning has already proven its strong capability of learning really complex correlations, and it might be better to leave correlation learning tasks at scale to product teams in the industry, in specific application scenarios.

So far, causal reasoning is a promising direction to strong AI, or at least it is a further step towards it compared to correlation learning. Reinforcement learning can be thought of having a little flavor of causal reasoning because it usually asks one question: what consequences will I get if I take this action under this context (\(Q(s,a)\))? RL also gives an agent the opportunity of interacting with its surrounding environment to acquire embodied knowledge, which I think is critical to strong AI.
